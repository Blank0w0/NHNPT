Redefining Urban Energy

Population-dense cities are obviously huge sources of power demand, consuming two-thirds of the world’s energy and producing a similar proportion of global carbon emissions. This is one of the many questions that could be answered by the ‘smart city’ concept that has taken root and garnered policy support in recent years. The smart city utilizes the available resources like data and digital connectivity to enhance the functionalites in managing sustainable green energy management.

ENERGY CATEGORIZATION

Local Resilience with Microgrids


The possibility to break the hegemony of a centralized distribution system and making energy more local and efficient is something which make the smart city concept of energy attractive to commoners. Replacing the traditional mode of utility structure with more efficient micro- grid based projects will result in a revolutionized form of entire energy sector. This in term will benefit by enabling decentralized distributed energy infrastructure that can improve system resilience.

Energy Communities

The role of microgrid is huge as it serves as enablers for local-based energy schemes opposed to traditional one. For example, theBrooklyn microgrid project is a collaboration between LO3 Energy and Siemens, which aims to introduce a microgrid-supported local energy market that gives residents with rooftop solar capacity to sell their excess to their neighbors. Smart grids will help distribution systems keep pace with the deeper integration of intermittent renewables such as wind and solar, and smart city projects are investigating how intelligent, connected local energy storage systems can support more renewables on the grid.

Efficiency and Demand-Side Response


On the other side of the coin, energy efficiency and demand-side management strategies represent a core part of re-balancing the smart city’s energy mix. Having support from city authorities is important. The energy transition also presents difficulties for those working to reduce urban energy demand. The rise of electric vehicles is an important part of decarbonization and air quality efforts, especially in cities, but handling the extra demand generated by tens of thousands of energy-hungry EVs and plug-in hybrids will be one of the key challenges for energy management in a smart city.

Financing Smart Cities


These smart city concepts were easy or cheap, everyone would be trying them. The fact is that grid modernization is a technical debt that utilities, often dealing with infrastructure that has moved far beyond its original lifecycle, have been struggling to pay for decades. Smart grid projects might offer long-term solutions to urban energy dilemmas, but many cities don’t have the money to make the big investments.

BENEFITS

01.

Green Energy Reliability

Green energy services can actually be more reliable than traditional energy services. Green energy systems, such as wind and solar power, are less likely to experience large-scale failure because they are distributed, environment-friendly and modular. That means problems in one geographical area, like severe weather, or problems with one piece of equipment won’t do as much to disrupt service to an entire region.

02.

Jobs

The growing appreciation for and technological development of green energy means that more jobs are being created in the renewable energy industry every day.

03.

Renewable Energy Lowers Healthcare Costs

It’s no secret that air and water pollution from fossil fuels can cause cancer, breathing problems, heart attacks, and other serious health issues. Replacing carbon-emitting fuels with green energy sources helps reduce harmful emissions and cleans up air and water quality, which improves public health and lowers overall healthcare costs.

04.

Economic Benefits of Green Energy

Most local governments collect taxes from renewable energy projects, which can be used to improve public services. New jobs directly created in the green energy industry create a ripple effect that benefits other related industries. Even businesses that are unrelated to the green energy industry often benefit from rising incomes across households and businesses.

05.

Green Energy Means Energy Independence

Because most green energy sources (like wind, sunlight, and water) don’t need to be imported, using green energy helps reduce our nation’s dependence on foreign imports, like oil. Even more than that, by using more green energy sources, many states can dramatically reduce their need to spend money importing coal and oil from across the country or from foreign countries, leading to greater energy independence.

06.

Renewable Energy is an Inexhaustible Energy Source

Green energy sources are renewable, meaning they are replenished naturally and relatively quickly. Natural resources like wind, water, sunlight, and geothermal heat could potentially supply the entire country’s energy needs with surplus. And unlike our finite supply of fossil fuels, renewable energy sources are inexhaustible and in continuous supply.
